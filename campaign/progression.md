# Progression And Rewards

Campaign id: `new_campaign`

Progression is how player action changes character capability, relationships,
access, reputation, and the world. Do not treat upgrade as a random prize. An
upgrade is the table-visible consequence of what the player actually did.

## Progression Philosophy

- Reward the behavior the campaign wants to see.
- Connect every meaningful reward to fiction: who taught it, what changed,
  what resource was gained, who noticed, and what future pressure answers it.
- Prefer new choices, access, identity, and world reaction over simple number
  inflation.
- Follow `play_profile.yaml.advancement.presentation`; closure never implies a
  mandatory OOC interlude by itself.
- Allied or companion NPCs can advance too, but their growth should follow
  their own participation, wounds, loyalties, training, and choices.
- Reward size is based on achievement quality first and play volume second.
- GM-awarded perks can recognize repeated play behavior, but they are not
  player-selected upgrades and cannot be exchanged for a different reward.

## Advancement Presentation Contract

`play_profile.yaml.advancement.presentation` is authoritative:

- `explicit_ooc`: when an earned advancement requires a player choice, offer a
  short table-facing interlude. Fiction may lock only while that choice is
  unresolved; the player may defer it and immediately resume play.
- `automatic_fictional`: express earned, already permitted advancement through
  fictional change without a mandatory OOC interlude, menu, or fiction lock.
  If consent or a player-authored choice is still needed, defer the upgrade
  without blocking narration rather than silently choosing for the player.
- `none`: create no advancement offer, OOC interlude, or advancement gate.
  Closure and its world/relationship consequences still occur normally.

The live interlude status and any temporary fiction lock exist only in
`arc_closure.md` Current Progression State. Historical closure entries record
what happened but do not create a second live gate.

## Closure Levels

A closure level establishes what changed and what could be earned; it never
creates an interlude or lock by itself. Treat each `Default reward` below as a
candidate only when cadence and presentation allow it.

### Beat

- Trigger: a small scene beat, clue, bargain, fight, or consequence resolves.
- Default reward: no formal upgrade; maybe a fictional opening, clue, contact
  reaction, or temporary edge.
- OOC check-in: usually none.

### Session

- Trigger: a play session or coherent scene chain ends.
- Default reward: minor milestone.
- Presentation-aware handling: under `explicit_ooc`, offer a quick review when
  a choice is due; under `automatic_fictional`, express an earned adjustment
  through play; under `none`, create no advancement gate.
- Good rewards: temporary edge, small resource, contact warmth, relationship
  shift, trait/aspect wording tweak, clue consolidation, or recovery space.

### Scenario

- Trigger: a mission, job, local problem, important threat, or clear objective
  closes.
- Default reward: significant milestone.
- Presentation-aware handling: offer 2 to 3 directions only under
  `explicit_ooc`; under `automatic_fictional`, apply only an already permitted,
  fiction-bound change without stopping play; under `none`, skip the offer.
- Good rewards: stat/skill improvement within budget, new or improved special
  capability, reputation tag, faction access, useful item, contact, companion
  bond, base access, map/lore unlock, or durable resource.

### Arc

- Trigger: a major antagonist, faction issue, personal conflict, island, route,
  or long dramatic question changes state.
- Default reward: major milestone.
- Presentation-aware handling: `explicit_ooc` may run a full choice review;
  `automatic_fictional` applies permitted growth and world response inside the
  fiction without a mandatory pause; `none` records closure/world response but
  opens no advancement gate.
- Good rewards: level band increase, stat cap/budget increase, signature
  capability growth, faction status, base/crew upgrade, companion promotion,
  world-state change, new route, public title, feared reputation, or major
  identity/trait change.

### Campaign

- Trigger: the whole campaign premise resolves or transforms.
- Default reward: legacy/epilogue advancement.
- Presentation-aware handling: ask explicit legacy choices only under
  `explicit_ooc` or by player request. `automatic_fictional` narrates permitted
  legacy changes without a forced interlude; `none` closes without an
  advancement gate.

## End-Of-Closure Review Questions

Review these privately at session, scenario, and arc closure. Ask them in
Designer/OOC table language only under `explicit_ooc` or when the player
requests a review. Under `automatic_fictional`, answer from established play;
under `none`, use only the questions needed to record world consequences.

- What did the player choose that changed the situation?
- What did the character learn about the world, themself, or another person?
- What threat, obstacle, debt, or pressure was overcome or transformed?
- What resource, contact, item, place, route, secret, or relationship was
  gained?
- What personal goal, belief, bond, flaw, or identity changed?
- What did the world notice?
- Which faction, enemy, ally, or companion now reacts differently?
- What new problem answers the character's growth?

## Reward Calibration

Use achievement quality as the main factor and play volume as a secondary
factor. A long arc with little meaningful achievement should not produce a
large power upgrade. A short arc with a decisive, costly, or transformative
achievement can still justify a strong reward.

### Play Volume

- Short: a brief session, small scene chain, or compact job.
- Medium: several substantial scenes or a normal scenario.
- Long: many turns, multiple sessions, or a large location/problem.
- Saga: a long-running arc with many linked scenes, relationships, and
  consequences.

### Achievement Quality

- Minor: color, survival, small information, small contact, or setup.
- Moderate: clear success, useful discovery, relationship shift, or local
  progress.
- Major: meaningful threat overcome, important social/faction shift, major
  discovery, costly victory, or durable access gained.
- Transformative: identity change, faction/world state shift, major enemy or
  issue transformed, life-changing discovery, or hard sacrifice.

### Reward Budget

- Low: temporary edge, small relationship warmth, small recovery, or minor
  fiction opening.
- Standard: one meaningful chosen upgrade or access/reputation/resource gain.
- High: chosen upgrade plus notable world/access/reputation or companion
  advancement.
- Exceptional: major capability or identity growth plus world-state response;
  reserve for long or transformative closure.

Genre fit matters. A social campaign rewards social access, trust, reputation,
and relationship leverage. An exploration campaign rewards routes, maps,
truths, safe havens, tools, and guides. A combat-heavy fantasy campaign may
reward techniques, spells, equipment, class-like growth, blessings, or renown.

## Player Motivation Lens

Use the player's visible preferences to make upgrades feel alive:

- power: new capability, stronger stat, better tool, clearer build path;
- tactics: new strategic option, enemy weakness, preparation asset;
- story: new thread, dramatic consequence, changed issue;
- roleplay: trait/aspect/bond change, relationship turn, inner conflict;
- exploration: new map, route, lore, secret, place, or truth;
- social: faction access, title, renown, fear, respect, contact network;
- collection: unique item, recipe, artifact, identity, ship/base feature;
- mastery: training, refinement, reliability, reduced cost, better counterplay.

Do not assume the motivation forever. Infer it from repeated player choices.
Ask during an OOC upgrade check-in only when presentation is `explicit_ooc`;
otherwise defer uncertain player-authored advancement without blocking play.

## Reward Pool

Pick rewards from the fiction first, then fit the category.

- mechanical_upgrade: stat, skill, level band, cap, or reliability increase;
- temporary_boost: one-use edge, inspiration, reroll, advantage, favor, or safe
  recovery window;
- new_ability: new special capability, technique, spell, hack, stunt, craft,
  contact method, or social power;
- reputation: fame, fear, wanted status, title, rumor, or public mark;
- faction_access: permission, safe passage, discount, agent, protection, or
  restricted information;
- base_upgrade: room, safehouse, ship, workshop, lab, shrine, office, crew
  support, or production capacity;
- companion_contact: new ally, improved bond, loyalty shift, or trained helper;
- item_artifact: tool, weapon, document, map, charm, device, resource, or
  symbol of authority;
- map_lore_unlock: route, truth, recipe, ritual, weakness, secret, or hidden
  location;
- trait_change: aspect, belief, goal, flaw, bond, scar, trauma, oath, or
  identity wording;
- world_state_change: issue resolved, location changed, faction move, law
  altered, route opened, enemy weakened, or threat escalated.

## Reward Package Structure

When the selected presentation permits an advancement package at scenario,
arc, or campaign closure, separate it into:

- Chosen upgrade: 2 to 3 options the player chooses between.
- World / access consequence: a fiction result the GM applies because the world
  changed.
- Companion / ally advancement: reviewed for any NPC who meaningfully
  participated or changed.
- GM-awarded perk: optional, based on repeated player behavior.

Under `explicit_ooc`, do not replace the chosen upgrade with a world
consequence or GM-awarded perk; they answer different parts of the closure.
Under `automatic_fictional`, omit the option menu and apply only a permitted,
fiction-bound upgrade. Under `none`, omit the advancement package while still
recording earned world and relationship consequences.

## Fiction Binding

Before finalizing any reward, answer:

- Where did it come from?
- Who gave, taught, unlocked, witnessed, or paid for it?
- What did it cost?
- Who is pleased, afraid, insulted, or threatened by it?
- How does it change the next session's pressure?
- Does it need downtime, training, recovery, crafting, travel, or a scene?

## Balance Check

Before presenting upgrade choices:

- Is this reward temporary or permanent?
- Is it individual, companion, faction, base, or world-level?
- Does it increase raw power, access, recognition, agency, or identity?
- Does it fit the closure level?
- Does it overshadow existing capabilities?
- Does the next threat or social pressure need to respond?
- Does it make the game richer instead of shorter?

## Companion And Ally Advancement

Companions and allied NPCs can advance when they:

- participated meaningfully;
- took risk or suffered cost;
- learned from the player or the situation;
- changed loyalty, confidence, role, reputation, or capability;
- gained equipment, access, training, or a new relationship.

Companion rewards may include:

- capability growth;
- clearer role in the group;
- improved trust or loyalty;
- new fear, scar, bond, oath, or ambition;
- faction access;
- equipment or resource;
- promotion from contact to companion or companion to major ally.

Do not make allied NPCs static reward containers. They keep agency. The player
may influence their growth, but the NPC's own motive and experience shape the
final result.

## GM-Awarded Perks

A GM-awarded perk is a small durable recognition of how the player actually
played. It is not selected from the upgrade menu and is not exchangeable for a
different reward.

Under `automatic_fictional`, introduce a permitted perk through its fiction
source without pausing for an OOC interlude. Under `none`, do not propose or
grant a progression perk.

Use a GM-awarded perk when the player repeatedly:

- solves problems through a consistent method;
- relies on a distinctive capability under pressure;
- builds a specific social reputation;
- accepts a recurring cost or risk;
- protects, teaches, deceives, studies, performs, crafts, explores, or fights
  in a pattern the fiction clearly recognizes.

Every perk needs:

- fiction source;
- trigger;
- limit;
- risk, counterplay, or situation where it does not apply.

The player may reject a perk for tone, consent, or character-concept reasons.
If rejected, do not convert it into another reward unless the GM decides the
fiction demands a different expression of the same behavior.

## Explicit-OOC Upgrade Check-In Script

Use this only when advancement presentation is `explicit_ooc` and a choice is
due. `automatic_fictional` and `none` must not invoke it automatically.

Use table-facing language:

"This arc closes here. Let's take a short advancement pause: what did you
change, what did you learn, and how do you want that to show up in the
character or the world?"

Then present 2 to 3 clear upgrade directions tied to what happened. Also state
any world/access consequence and any GM-awarded perk separately. Avoid
technical file language.
