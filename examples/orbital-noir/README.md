# Example Seed: Orbital Noir

This is a generic starter seed for testing RePoG. It is not a complete
campaign and does not depend on any copyrighted setting.

## Pitch

A decaying orbital city survives by selling logistics, silence, and legal
ambiguity to everyone passing through the inner system. The player is a private
fixer, investigator, courier, or disgraced security officer taking small jobs
that keep touching a larger station-wide cover-up.

## Session 0 Answers

- Genre: sci-fi noir.
- Tone: tense, human, practical, morally grey.
- Scale: one orbital city, with later access to ships and nearby colonies.
- Play focus: investigation, social leverage, access, favors, surveillance,
  and risk-managed action.
- Technology level: advanced but unevenly distributed.
- Canon policy: original setting.
- Avoid: superheroic combat, instant hacking miracles, endless corporate
  monologues.
- Campaign length: long-form, built around cases that reveal station pressure.

## World Notes

- Air, docking rights, storage, and legal identity are all political tools.
- The station council is public; the real power sits in maintenance contracts.
- Most residents are tired, busy, and careful with information.
- A missing cargo container matters because of who is pretending not to look
  for it.
- The station's old systems remember more than the current owners want.

## Player Seed

The player character starts with a small office, one reliable contact, one
enemy in station administration, and one professional line they try not to
cross.

Recommended starting level: Rising / Competent.

Suggested capabilities:

- surveillance tradecraft;
- social access and negotiation;
- station systems expertise;
- close-quarters defense;
- legal or customs knowledge;
- shipboard repair.

Each capability should include a cost, limit, countermeasure, and social risk.

## First Scene Brief

The character is waiting in a late-night customs queue when a dockworker they
barely know slips them a dead access token and says, "If anyone asks, I never
made it this far."

The scene should show normal station life first: tired travelers, bored
inspectors, humming vents, overdue announcements. The hook should be present
but not consume the whole room.

## Expected Play Style

Avoid making every NPC cryptic. Let people be tired, practical, scared,
greedy, kind, or distracted. Important clues can appear as paperwork,
contradictions, camera gaps, habits, favors, and physical access, not only as
dialogue.
